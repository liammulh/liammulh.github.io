About this blog and its author, Liam Mulhall

Welcome to my humble digital abode. My name is Liam. I'm a software engineer based in the San Francisco Bay Area.

About this blog

I write expository prose on what I'm learning to clarify concepts in my mind. This blog isn't meant to be an authoritative source of information. Rather, it's a dynamic space for intellectual exploration.

I occasionally write reviews for books, movies, board games, etc.

About me

My background

I was born and raised in Colorado's Roaring Fork Valley. I attended the University of Colorado Boulder, where in May of 2022 I obtained a bachelor's degree in mathematics with a specialization in computation, complemented by a minor in computer science.

My career thus far

I'm a software engineer working on the Clinical Genome Resource (ClinGen) project housed in Stanford University's school of medicine. ClinGen is an initiative funded by the National Institutes of Health dedicated to constructing a central repository elucidating the clinical significance of genes and variants for precision medicine and research. In my role at ClinGen, I craft and maintaining both front-end and back-end features for ClinGen's gene curation interface and its variant curation interface, tools employed by genetics researchers to standardize the curation of genetic information.

Before ClinGen, I worked at PhET Interactive Simulations, an education non-profit housed within the University of Colorado's physics department. PhET's mission revolves around the creation of free, interactive math and science simulations, optimized for web browsers, designed to engage students through an intuitive, game-like environment. My responsibilities at PhET included spearheading the development and maintenance of a web application used to translate PhET simulations from English into other languages.

I've also lent my expertise to Numberscope, a software project housed within the University of Colorado Boulder's Experimental Mathematics Lab whose aim is to provide a browser-based tool for visualizing integer sequences like the Fibonacci sequence. My contributions encompassed diverse facets, such as upgrading dependencies, conducting rigorous testing, crafting comprehensive documentation, and performing server administration, in addition to resolving back-end bugs.

My project portfolio

In the summer of 2023, I began work on Underwood, a static blog generator initially conceived as a modest Bash script but ultimately metamorphosed into a Python package that now graces the Python Package Index (PyPI). This blog was generated using Underwood.

In the fall of 2022, I made 0ty, a static website generator leveraging Node.js. Its development concluded in the spring of 2023, and it made its home on the NPM registry. I abandoned it in favor of Underwood.

In the fall of 2018, I took a class on data structures at CU Boulder in which I wrote a neural network from scratch in C++ for my final project. This neural network was trained to behave like an exclusive or (XOR) gate.

My expertise

In obtaining my degree in pure mathematics, I learned how to approach abstract problems with creative yet methodical thinking. I also learned how to identify and exploit patterns in abstract problems. Both of these skills have proved invaluable across many domains.

As a software engineer, I've learned the art of decomposing complex problems into manageable components. I've also learned how to design abstractions, balance constraints, and communicate effectively in writing and speech.
